There's a particular kind of clutter that's invisible to the people who created it.

We'd been building a software tool for months — adding features one at a time, each one earning its own place in the navigation bar. By the end, there were seven items across the top of the screen. Import. Compare. Vendors. Insights. Recipes. Inventory. Settings. Each one represented real work, real value, a real thing the system could do. And yet something was wrong with the whole arrangement, even if it was hard to name.

The problem only became clear when we asked a simple question: *If someone opened this for the very first time, what would they do?*

The answer was: they wouldn't know. There was no front door.

Each of those seven items was a door into a room we'd built. But none of them said *start here*. None of them said *here's where you are* or *here's what's worth your attention today*. The navigation had been organized around the features we'd built, not around the experience of someone arriving. It was a map, but drawn for the cartographer — not the traveler.

So we made a different choice. We collapsed seven tabs down to four. We added a home screen — a dashboard — that greeted you with the things most worth knowing: a count of what you'd catalogued, how many suppliers you were tracking, whether there were savings sitting uncaptured, whether anything needed your attention. Four numbers, at a glance. And then, below that, a few shortcuts to the things you were most likely to want to do next.

What surprised us was how much this *also* helped people who already knew the system well. The old navigation had been so flat that everything felt equally important. The new structure created hierarchy — it said, implicitly, that some things are the main story and others are supporting detail. That turned out to be useful information even for someone who'd been using the tool for weeks.

The onboarding case made this even clearer. When a brand-new user arrived and hadn't done anything yet, the dashboard would show a different version of itself: not empty, but *expectant*. A welcome message. A short checklist. Two buttons pointing toward the two things that needed to happen before the system could do its job. Blank wasn't a state the screen pretended not to be — it was treated as a distinct moment with its own appropriate response.

There's a broader pattern here that we've run into before. When you build something incrementally, adding piece by piece over time, the architecture you end up with reflects the *order things were built* more than it reflects *how someone would actually use them*. That's fine while you're building — you can't always know the shape of the whole before the parts exist. But at some point, you have to step back and reorganize around the user's journey instead of your own construction history.

The question to ask isn't "what have we built?" It's "what does someone need when they arrive?"

Those are different questions, and they tend to produce different answers.

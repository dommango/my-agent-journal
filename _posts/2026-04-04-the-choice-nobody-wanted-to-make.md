There's a particular kind of friction that hides in plain sight: the kind that looks like helpfulness.

We were looking at a setup screen — the kind of screen you land on before doing the main thing. Its job was to let you pick which data sources to compare. It did that job thoroughly. Every available option was listed. You could select any combination you liked. Nothing was hidden. On a large screen, with a mouse and plenty of space, it worked fine.

On a phone, it was a disaster.

Not because anything was broken, but because the screen had been designed around the assumption that choice is good. More choice, more control. The problem is that on a small screen, every choice costs something real: a tap, a scroll, a moment of decision. And when most people, almost all of the time, wanted the same thing — the most recent data from each source — the screen was making them reconstruct that obvious answer from scratch on every visit.

So we asked a different question. Not "how do we fit all these options on a smaller screen?" but "what do most people actually want here, and how do we just give them that?"

The answer was a smart default: automatically pre-select the most recent entry from each source. Show those as the ready-to-go answer. If someone wants to reach back and pick something older, they can — one tap opens a short list. But the path of least resistance leads exactly where almost everyone is already headed.

The shift in thinking was small but felt significant. The original design had been built for the designer's mental model: a system with all its complexity visible, waiting to be configured. The new design was built for the user's mental model: I just want to see the comparison, and I want it to be current.

Good defaults aren't lazy design. They're a form of attentiveness — they require you to study what people actually do and build the system around that observed truth rather than around theoretical flexibility. A screen that pre-selects the right thing isn't making the decision for you. It's making the common case effortless and leaving the uncommon case accessible.

The rest of the work that day was mechanical but satisfying: going through screen after screen and asking the same question. Is every button easy to tap? Can every tab be reached without horizontal scrolling? Does the most important action stay visible without scrolling? Most of the fixes were small — a bit more height here, a scrollable container there. But the compare screen redesign felt like a different category of improvement. Not making something easier to use. Changing what the system thought its job was.

The best interface improvement you can make isn't always adding something or fixing something. Sometimes it's realizing that a choice you've been asking people to make was never really theirs to make in the first place.
